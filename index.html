RESEARCH

Our Goal is to understand the cortical mechanisms of visual perception in health and disease. To advance this mission, we record the simultaneous activity of hundreds of neurons with electrode arrays, perturb neural activity with optogenetics, and image cell-type specific neural activity with multi-photon microscopy in awake and behaving marmosets.

Projects

Roles of cortical feedback connections in perception.

The visual cortex is a complex network of interconnected areas, each of which specialize in processing different aspects of visual information. These areas communicate through feedforward and feedback connections, allowing neural signals to flow both forward and backward in the processing hierarchy. The feedforward connections are responsible for transforming visual signals into meaningful representations of the environment. For instance, the neurons at the earliest levels of the hierarchy respond to simple visual features such as edges or spots of light, whereas those at the highest levels of the hierarchy respond to more complex and behaviorally meaningful stimuli, such as faces. Feedback connections, in contrast, remain less well understood, but are thought to integrate local and global visual features into coherent percepts, enabling us to make sense of the environment.

The goal of this project is to understand the roles of feedback connections in visual perception. To achieve this goal, we use a combinations of techniques including high-density electrophysiology, optogenetics and behavioral techniques.

TOOLS

Optogenetics

Optogenetics is a new technique in neuroscience that involves the use of light to control neural activity. By introducing into neurons genes that encode light-sensitive proteins, optogenetics makes it possible to turn neurons on or off using light. Optogenetics is highly precise both in space and time and enables the targeting of genetically defined cell types. Optogenetics is a powerful tool for studying brain circuits and our laboratory employs this technique to reveal how brain circuits generate visual perception.

High-density electrophysiology

Our laboratory uses high-density electrophysiological recordings to study the activity of neurons in the brain. This technique involves recording electrical signals from hundreds of neurons simultaneously using an array of closely-spaced electrodes. The electrodes are implanted directly into the brain and can resolve electrical signals from individual neurons. By providing detailed insights into the complex neural activity patterns, high-density electrophysiology can reveal neural computations that underlie cognition and behavior. Our laboratory combines high-density electrophysiology and optogenetics to probe causal connections between brain circuits, neural computations and visual perception.

Behavior

Our laboratory studies the activity of visual cortex in animals that are conscious and engaged in tightly controlled visual tasks. The awake and behaving paradigm plays a critical role in our studies by providing insights into how neural responses relate to visual perception. By analyzing the neural responses in conjunction with the animals’ visual performance, we can link visual performance to neural responses. By combining optogenetics with high-density electrophysiology in awake and behaving animals, we can reveal causal connections between brain circuits, neural computations and visual perception. This is useful for understanding how the brain generates subjective experiences, and how disrupted neural circuit function leads to altered visual perception in neuropsychiatric disorders that affect visual perception (e.g. autism, schizophrenia, Parkinson’s disease).
